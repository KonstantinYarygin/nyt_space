BY By MARK LANDLER

FRANKFURT -- TO judge by the economic surveys that are published almost daily in Europe, the Continent is solidly on the road to recovery. So why are the people who study these barometers so dour?

The trend is clear: Germany's influential Ifo index of business sentiment rose in September for the fifth consecutive month, to its highest level in more than two years.

TD 

The ZEW survey of investor confidence, released by the ZEW Center for European Economic Research, and its consumer counterpart, the GfK survey, by the market research company GfK in Nuremberg, are also showing newfound vigor.

The upward-pointing arrows are not limited to Germany. In France, the closely watched Insee survey, by the national statistics office, recorded its second consecutive monthly improvement last week, after a long stretch of gloomy results.

Yet rather than proclaim a recovery under way, most economists warn that it is too soon to declare a turn in Europe's fortunes. Part of the caution stems from experience. Most of these surveys registered similar gains in sentiment last year, stoking hopes of a recovery that never materialized. Germany fell back into a mild recession last autumn, while the rest of Europe stagnated. ''People had their fingers burned,'' said Thomas Mayer, the chief European economist at Deutsche Bank.

Hope may again be sprinting ahead of reality. ''It's too early to say a recovery has arrived,'' Mr. Mayer said. ''For that, we'd like to see a turnaround in current conditions. Unfortunately, there is a considerable disparity between expectations and conditions.''

Consider the Ifo index, which is published by the Ifo Institute, an economic research group in Munich. The index, a survey of 7,000 German companies, showed an improvement in sentiment in September. But it was driven by a rise in expectations for a better future, not on conditions today.

With the exception of a positive report on manufacturing orders last month, there is no tangible evidence -- in statistics like unemployment or retail sales -- that the German economy is moving. The disparity between emotions and evidence exists in France and other countries.

On the same day the new Insee survey was published, French officials lowered their estimates of gross domestic product in late 2002 and early 2003, which means their already modest goal of 0.5 percent growth this year is probably unattainable.

Some experts chalk this up to the normal cycle of an economic recovery. There was a similar lag between improved sentiment and tangible economic results in the United States, they said. ''Europe is at a much earlier stage,'' said David Abramson, the chief European strategist at the BCA Research Group in Montreal. Beyond issues of timing, however, the caution reflects a fear that this recovery is unusually fragile. The euro has renewed its climb against the dollar, which will put pressure on the German and French exports that are considered a crucial driver of any sustained growth in Europe.

''The euro is one of the major threats to the recovery,'' said Michael Heise, the chief economist at Allianz in Frankfurt. A sharply higher euro, he noted, could cause Europe's economies to succumb yet again.

Mr. Heise said he was encouraged that the German government had pushed ahead with politically painful reforms of the labor market, as well as of welfare and pension benefits. The government also cheered the public by accelerating a tax cut that had been scheduled for 2005.

The trouble is, too much good news on the economic front could pose a threat to the reform agenda of Chancellor Gerhard Schroder. European leaders have tended to put their reform plans in mothballs during times of robust growth because they no longer appear as necessary.

PERHAPS the easiest way to deal with the curse of rising expectations would be to pay less attention to the alphabet soup of surveys. Ifo, ZEW, and GfK came to prominence partly because of the growth of financial markets. Investors like trading on the numbers.

But they also reflect the tardiness and unreliability of the economic data released by European governments. If Paris, Berlin and Rome issued timely, dependable statistics, Mr. Mayer said, the influence of these private surveys would ebb.

As it is, he said, the surveys can even distort an economic cycle. Last year, after the flood of upbeat reports, the European Central Bank sent out hawkish signals about interest rates. That spooked investors, whose reactions may have contributed to the subsequent relapse of the economy.

''We need a more balanced diet,'' Mr. Mayer said. ''If we live off these surveys, we'll get indigestion.''

Consumer confidence rose this month as a measure of current economic conditions increased to its highest level in more than a year, a University of Michigan survey showed yesterday.

The university's preliminary July sentiment index rose to 90.3 from 89.7 last month. That was the third rise in four months. The Standard & Poor's index of 500 stocks has risen 22 percent since a low in March, which may have contributed to higher confidence this month, economists said.

TD 

Lower interest rates and prospects of tax cuts may also be increasing optimism, paving the way for stronger consumer spending and growth that is needed before companies feel confident enough to resume hiring.

''What we are going to have is good growth, but it's not going to be a boom,'' said John Silvia, chief economist at the Wachovia Corporation in Charlotte, N.C. ''Consumers still have a lot of concerns out there. I don't see how consumers are going to write off the war or write off the job problem.''

The current conditions index, which reflects the perceptions of Americans of their financial situation and whether it is a good time to make major purchases, rose to 102.1, the highest point since May 2002. It was 94.7 in June.

The second half of the year ''will be significantly better than the first half,'' Thomas Dattilo, chief executive of the Cooper Tire & Rubber Company, the nation's second-biggest tire maker, said in an interview yesterday. ''There is going to be more money in consumers' pockets; the tax cuts will make sure of that. We certainly see an improved sentiment out there in retail.''

TOKYO, July 1 -- Pessimism about the Japanese economy is slowly fading, a central bank survey of business executives showed today, as manufacturers prepare for an increase in orders from overseas for their cars, electronics and machinery.

The Bank of Japan's quarterly ''tankan'' report suggests that concerns about the world's second-largest economy sliding into recession are overblown. Though the large manufacturers that typically drive economic recoveries here remain wary, they are quietly raising their profit and capital spending goals.

TD 

The poll's index of major manufacturers rose five points to minus 5, which means pessimists still outnumbered optimists but by a smaller margin. The companies' financial outlooks were stronger than the predictions of private economists, who expected confidence to slip.

The improvement, however mild, comes as disruptions from the SARS virus and the war in Iraq fade, while oil prices slide and stock prices extend a two-month rally. The United States economy is also improving, good news for exporters like Honda Motor, the Matsushita Electric Industrial Company and other companies that earn most of their profits overseas.

While corporate managers expect no improvement in confidence by the time of the next survey in September, manufacturers raised their pretax profit forecasts for the fiscal year that began in April. Some of that progress comes as companies reduce their payrolls, close factories and streamline balance sheets.

But manufacturers are also preparing for new sales by planning to invest more in factories and operations. Large manufacturers, for instance, expect their capital spending to increase 11.5 percent this year.

''The economy is still at the mercy of exports,'' said Ryo Hino, an economist at J. P. Morgan Securities in Tokyo. ''But the numbers suggest that the momentum is stronger than we thought.''

Japan's chief financial regulator, Heizo Takenaka, said the survey's results were within expectations, while the finance minister, Masajuro Shiokawa, said the improvement at large manufacturers was ''an honest reflection'' of their restructuring efforts.

Investors viewed today's news as a bullish sign for the economy. The Nikkei 225 stock index rose 2.2 percent to 9,278, a nine-month high. The dollar slipped against the yen after the report was released, but later recouped its losses.

The tankan's optimism was seconded by a separate report that showed new car sales rising 2.6 percent in June, the ninth gain in 10 months.

Though Japan's $4.2 trillion economy is showing signs of life, the recovery is far from robust. Domestic sales remain sluggish, and residents face a difficult job market, falling incomes and higher taxes.

The main culprit is deflation, or falling prices. While price declines help consumers in search of cheaper goods, they hurt the companies that make those products.

To compensate, companies must reduce hiring and cut other fixed costs.

The worst-hit companies are those that compete with low-cost producers from China, South Korea and other Asian countries. While demand for cellphones, personal digital assistants and other electronics are strong, the prices of the components packed into these machines remain weak.

''I don't think prices will improve this year because of the macroeconomy,'' said Yoshihide Fujii, executive vice president in charge of semiconductors at Toshiba, Japan's largest chipmaker. ''We have only one choice: To cut costs to cope with this.''

Though the government has put together several antideflation spending packages and the Bank of Japan has followed an ultra-loose monetary policy for several years, prices are locked in their fifth year of decline.

HD  Bucking the Tide, eBay Expands in Silicon Valley
BY  By TERRY PRISTIN
WC 1423 words

SAN JOSE, Calif., May 8 -- Last month, eBay, the Internet auction company, brought some welcome news to a city and region that have been suffering from one of the worst office market slumps in the nation. The eight-year-old company, which had toyed with giving up its headquarters here, announced that it would not only keep its West San Jose complex but would also buy a 32-acre campus from the software developer Novell on the opposite side of town.

The eBay deal, which still must undergo an environmental review, would allow the company to expand gradually, thereby avoiding the mistakes made by other highflying Silicon Valley companies that overcommitted themselves during the economic boom of the late 1990's and are now stuck with empty or only partly occupied buildings because of the slide in the technology industry.

TD 

Almost half of the 511,000 square feet of space in the five aluminum-and-steel buildings of the Novell campus is leased, leaving just 260,000 square feet for eBay to occupy in the immediate future. The company won several unusually generous concessions from the city that will allow further expansion to as much as two million square feet.

But should eBay stop growing, said the city's economic development director, Paul Krutko, the company will be able to sell its development rights. ''This deal also provides an exit strategy for eBay,'' Mr. Krutko said.

Though the eBay deal lifted spirits, especially among city officials, it will have little effect on vacancy rates in the Silicon Valley, which by its broadest definition includes Santa Clara County, where San Jose is located; San Mateo County; and Fremont, in southern Alameda County. Santa Clara County alone has lost 200,000 jobs since 2000, about half of the job loss in the entire state. Evidence of the downturn is everywhere -- from the empty parking lots in front of office parks that are only a few years old, to the quiet atmosphere of downtown San Jose and the easily flowing traffic along freeways that were once congested.

A newly completed 17-story blue-and-tan skyscraper near the San Jose Convention Center that can be seen for miles has no tenants for its 380,000 square feet, and Boston Properties has shelved plans to put up three buildings across the street with a total 850,000 square feet, said Bob Pester, the regional manager for the developer.

Of the valley's 166.7 million square feet of space for high-technology research and development, 21.3 percent was vacant as of the end of the last quarter, compared with 2.3 percent for the peak of the market in the second quarter of 2000, said Charles W. Harry Jr., the managing director of the analytics advisory group for Cushman & Wakefield, the national real estate company. The vacancy rate for the office market of about 70.9 million square feet stands at 27.8 percent, compared with 1.8 percent three years ago, he said. The vacancy rate for the high-technology market is the worst it has been since the second quarter of 1993 while the office market has not been this soft since the first quarter of 1987, Mr. Harry said.

The numbers may not be the worst ever, but with 14 million square feet of high-technology space added in just a few years, many people in the industry expect the recovery to take a frustratingly long time. ''This is the worst market in the Silicon Valley that we've ever seen,'' said Raymond W. Marino, the president of Mission West Properties, a publicly traded real estate investment trust that manages 7.8 million square feet, primarily in Silicon Valley. ''In all prior downturns, we've always had a new company that was just coming into its own. This time, everyone is just stalled.''

Average annual asking rents in the research and development market were $15.41 a square foot in the last quarter, compared with a peak of $57.13 at the end of 2000, Mr. Harry said. In the office market, rents have dropped to $27.34 from a height of $69.68, according to Cushman & Wakefield. C. Michael Kamm, the chief executive of BT Commercial, a Northern California company, said ''rents have fallen to levels we haven't seen in seven or eight years.''

As their need for space evaporated, some companies paid dearly to get out of their leases. It cost Handspring, the personal organizer manufacturer, $75 million to extricate itself from plans to move from Mountain View to Sunnyvale, said Brian Jaquot, a company spokesman.

But in Silicon Valley, as in other high-technology areas, the typical lease runs only 5 to 7 years rather than 10 to 15 years. So while space available for sublease accounts for a substantial portion of the vacancies right now -- 13.2 million of the 35 million square feet of empty research-and-development space, according to BT Commercial -- many of those leases will expire in a year or two, shifting the burden for filling the space from tenants to landlords.

In anticipation, many landlords and tenants are working deals to ''blend and extend'' their leases, in brokers' jargon. Mr. Marino said he was working out one such deal with a San Jose technology company that needed only 45,000 of the 120,000 square feet that it had leased until September 2004. Under the proposed new terms, the company would extend its lease for the 45,000 square feet at market rates through May 31, 2008, and the $2.3 million in rent due on the remaining 75,000 square feet would be spread out (with an undisclosed amount of interest added) over the five-year term.

One measure of how bad things are, said James J. Curtis III, a partner in Bristol Group, a real estate investment company, is that some buildings used to command annual leases of $70 a square foot (without utilities); now space is being sold at the same price.

That is slightly more than the South Bay Development Company of Campbell, Calif., paid for an empty 359,000-square-foot building in the city of Santa Clara that it acquired in August from Nortel Networks. Built in 1975, the building was completely remodeled in 1999 at a cost of $100 a square foot, said Mark J. Regoli, a South Bay partner.

In November, his company bought a 78,000-square-foot property in Sunnyvale for $5 million; the seller, Qwest communications, had paid $25.2 million for the building the previous December, he said. ''For us,'' Mr. Regoli said, ''we view the next 6 to 24 months as a good opportunity to accumulate.''

Real estate prices have fallen so far that the 3Com Corporation, a leading maker of computer-networking equipment, recently took its 900,000-square-foot Santa Clara campus off the market. Brokers and investors said the company could not even get an offer of $100 a square foot, about one-third of what the complex cost to build. EBay has not disclosed what it paid for the landscaped Novell campus, which includes a reflecting pool; tennis, volleyball and basketball courts; jogging paths; a gymnasium; and an espresso cafe as well as a cafeteria. But one real estate specialist familiar with the negotiations said it fetched just under $185 a square foot.

San Jose city officials were so eager to keep eBay in town that they abandoned their plans to buy an adjacent 15-acre vacant site from Novell to use in connection with a major airport expansion program. Instead, Novell will sell the land to eBay.

As an Internet company, eBay does not generate sales tax revenue for San Jose, but it has 1,500 employees who spend their paychecks locally, Mr. Krutko said. ''EBay is what you call a 'gazelle company,' '' Mr. Krutko said, ''It doubles in size every five years. There are very few of those companies around.''

German business sentiment declined last month, the Ifo Institute said in a monthly report on one of Europe's most widely watched economic indicators. The decline, to 88.2 from 88.9, was the first in three months and disappointed analysts who were looking for a sign of expansion. Analysts said the decline was a result of corporate reluctance to make investments in light of uncertainty created by the war in Iraq. Petra Kappl (NYT)
